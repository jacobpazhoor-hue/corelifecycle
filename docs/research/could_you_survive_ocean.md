# Research — Could You Survive Alone in the Middle of the Ocean?  (survival format)

Format: **survival** ("Could You Survive [X]?"). The "levels" are a MONOTONIC TIME/THREAT ladder
(HOUR 0 → HOUR 1 → HOUR 6 → DAY 2 → DAY 4 → DAY 9 → DAY 14 → DAY 31 → DAY 54). The gold "$" overlay
becomes a **survival stat** at each stage (a countdown time, a body temp, a distance, a day count),
while the sub-caption tracks the collapsing resource / rising threat. The color grade escalates by
each level-start's position, so the mixed units are fine — the ladder just has to keep moving forward.

Second-person, present-tense POV: you are delivering a 40-foot sailboat across the open Pacific with
one friend (RAF). A late-season storm rolls and dismasts the boat 1,200 miles from land; you get into
a 4-person life raft with a ditch bag, and the ocean spends the next fifty-four days taking everything
you have, one system at a time — heat, then water, then hope, then Raf — before it decides whether to
set you down. This is a DRAMATIZED cautionary survival POV, not a how-to.

## The threat ladder (the on-screen spine) — all mechanics REAL unless FLAGGED

### HOUR 1 — COLD SHOCK / DROWNING  (overlay: "10 MIN")
- **1-10-1 rule** (Prof. Gordon Giesbrecht, cold-water immersion): ~**1 minute** of cold-shock
  response (involuntary gasp reflex + hyperventilation — the biggest immediate drowning risk; a gasp
  underwater is fatal), ~**10 minutes** of meaningful movement before your hands and arms stop obeying
  you (cold incapacitation), ~**1 hour** before hypothermia drops you into unconsciousness. REAL.
- The **gasp reflex**: sudden immersion forces an uncontrollable inhale — get your head clear first. REAL.
- Get OUT of the water / into the raft ASAP; water pulls body heat ~25× faster than air. REAL (≈25×).

### HOUR 6 — THE FIRST NIGHT / HYPOTHERMIA  (overlay: "95°F")
- Normal core temp **98.6°F**; **hypothermia begins below 95°F** (shivering, then confusion,
  then the shivering STOPS — a bad sign). REAL.
- **EPIRB / PLB** (emergency beacon) is the real lifeline — but only if it deployed, was registered,
  and someone is receiving; ditch-bag gear (flares, water, a solar still, fishing line) is what you
  live on. If the beacon is lost or dead, no one knows where you are. REAL.
- Nobody expects a delivery crew for **weeks** — the search may not even start in time. REAL premise.

### DAY 2 — THIRST  (overlay: "3 DAYS")
- **Rule of threes** (survival heuristic, approximate): ~3 minutes without air, ~**3 days without
  water**, ~3 weeks without food. FLAG: a rule of thumb, not a hard limit — varies with heat/exertion.
- **DO NOT DRINK SEAWATER.** Seawater is ~3.5% salt; your kidneys need MORE fresh water to flush that
  salt than the seawater provides, so drinking it **accelerates** dehydration (hypernatremia →
  delirium → death). This is the counter-intuitive **"wait, that's real?" share beat.** REAL.
- **Solar still** (evaporate seawater under clear plastic, collect the fresh condensate) + **rain
  catch** off the canopy are the real water sources; Steven Callahan used solar stills for 76 days. REAL.

### DAY 4 — THE SHIP THAT DOESN'T SEE YOU  (overlay: "600 M")
- A drifting life raft is **tiny and low** — from a ship's bridge a metre of orange canopy in open
  swell is nearly invisible, and no one watches the water **astern**. Ships pass within a mile and
  never know. This is the classic crushing false-hope beat. REAL.
- Flares are few and burn seconds; a **signal mirror / dye / SART** help only by day and only if
  someone's looking. REAL.

### DAY 9 — WHAT'S BELOW  (overlay: "DAY 9")
- **Mahi-mahi (dorado)**, triggerfish, and small pelagics gather under any floating object (a raft is
  a **FAD** — fish-aggregating device); you can catch them with a hand line and gaff — your food. REAL.
- The same shade draws **sharks**; oceanic whitetips and others investigate and bump a drifting raft;
  they can puncture the buoyancy tubes. Callahan's raft was struck repeatedly. REAL.
- Salt-water sores, sunburn, and immersion break the skin down within days. REAL.

### DAY 14 — ALONE (the midpoint)  (overlay: "DAY 14")
- Dehydration + exposure kill a weakened raftmate; grief + the sudden HALVING of morale is itself a
  survival threat. The raft that held two now holds one. (Dramatized; the physiology is real.)

### DAY 31 — THE STORM / THE TEAR  (overlay: "DAY 31")
- A second storm, a shark bump, or UV rot can **tear the buoyancy tube**; you bail with a shoe/cup
  and patch with the raft's kit while it slowly deflates. Callahan patched his raft for weeks. REAL.
- **Hallucination**: prolonged dehydration, sleeplessness, and isolation produce vivid hallucinations
  and a "third-man" felt-presence (documented in survivors — Shackleton, Slocum). REAL.

### DAY 54 — THE LIGHT (the payoff)  (overlay: "DAY 54")
- Rescue at sea usually comes from a **fishing vessel** or a ship on a shipping lane, or by drifting
  into land on a current — rarely from the search that was launched for you. Bittersweet by design.

## Real long-drift survivors (grounding + the record share beat)
- **José Salvador Alvarenga** — ~**438 days** adrift across the Pacific (Nov 2012 → Jan 2014), fishing
  boat disabled off Mexico, drifted ~9,000 mi to the Marshall Islands; the **longest known** solo
  ocean survival. His companion died on the way. FLAG: some details disputed, but the landing and
  duration are documented and widely reported. Use as the "a man once did this for 438 days" beat.
- **Steven Callahan** — **76 days** in a 6-foot life raft, Atlantic, 1982 ("Adrift"); solar stills,
  speared fish, patched the raft, rescued by fishermen off Guadeloupe. REAL.
- **Poon Lim** — **133 days** alone on a wooden raft after his ship was torpedoed, 1942–43, South
  Atlantic; long held the record. REAL.
- **Tami Oldham Ashcraft** — **41 days** sailing a dismasted, half-wrecked yacht to Hawaii after
  Hurricane Raymond, 1983 ("Red Sky in Mourning" / the film *Adrift*); her fiancé was lost. REAL.
- **Maurice & Maralyn Bailey** — **117 days** adrift in a raft/dinghy after a whale holed their yacht,
  1973, Pacific. REAL.

## Characters / story spine (see content.py PROMISE→PAYOFF ledger)
- **RAF (Rafael)** — your friend and delivery crewmate; the one other body in the raft; dies at the
  midpoint (DAY 14). His charge before he goes is the pre-midpoint dialogue.
- **ISLA** — your seven-year-old daughter on land; the reason-to-live anchor. Her line "Dad, don't be
  late" is the hope motif; the payoff is getting home to her (and never being all-the-way back).
- **Your father** — a dead commercial fisherman; his RULE ("The sea gives nothing back. Don't chase
  it — wait, and it sets you down.") is the mentor's warning (act-1 dialogue) and the thing that keeps
  you rationing and still. His brass dive knife on a lanyard is the one carried object.
- **Sensory anchor (body-based, per the trillionaire lesson — no undrawn prop):** THIRST + the taste
  of salt on cracked, splitting lips, re-triggered at every stage; secondary the raft's slow HISS
  (a leak = a countdown) and the father's knife (the days notched into the tube).
- **Unresolved universe thread (deliberate, left open):** the **SANTA ELENA** — a half-sunk fishing
  panga you drift past around DAY 9 with a cooler of water and a child's sandal aboard and no bodies;
  you take the water (therefore you live) and never learn who they were or whether anyone came for them.
