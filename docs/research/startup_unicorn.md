# Research — Every Level of a Startup (From Garage to $1B Unicorn)

**Framing (per topic_queue):** this is the COMPANY's ladder, not the founder's — `founder.md` already
told the founder's own money story. This episode's protagonist is instead a composite EARLY EMPLOYEE
(hire #1, an engineer) who rides the company's valuation climb as an owner of common stock/options,
never the person raising the money. The on-screen BIG number is the company's valuation at each
funding stage (same escalation spine as `founder.md`, verified there); the SUB-CAPTION is the
employee's OWN paper/real stake — that split (company value vs. your value) is the actual drama,
because dilution + liquidation preferences mean the two numbers do not move together.

## The ladder (company valuation = big; your stake = sub)

| # | Level | Stage | Company value (big) | Your stake (sub) | Mechanic |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | The Garage Hire | pre-seed, hire #1 | **$6,000,000** | **1.5%** ≈ $90,000 paper | initial grant, 4yr vest/1yr cliff |
| 2 | The Seed Crew | seed round | **$15,000,000** | ~1.2% ≈ $180,000 paper | diluted by the round |
| 3 | Series A | product-market fit | **$78,000,000** | ~1.0% ≈ $770,000 paper | "paper millionaire," still zero cash |
| 4 | The Scale-up | Series B/C, growth-at-all-costs | **$400,000,000** | ~0.77% ≈ $3,000,000 paper | first layoffs; moral cost begins |
| — | **THE DOWN ROUND (reversal)** | re-priced financing | **$420,000,000** (down from a rumored $1B) | **effectively $0 realizable** | 1x participating liquidation preference stacks new money ahead of common |
| 5 | Unicorn | crosses $1B | **$1,000,000,000** | ~0.63% ≈ $6,000,000 paper | cram-down dilution in the down round |
| 6 | The Tender | secondary sale window | (same $1B) | **$628,000 CASH** (real, wired) | employee tender offer — first real liquidity |
| 7 | The IPO | rings the bell | **$8,000,000,000** market cap | **~$40,000,000 paper** | 180-day lock-up — can't sell yet |
| 8 | After the Lock-up | apex, real number | (post-lockup price, lower) | **~$19,000,000 after tax** | AMT/ISO tax bite + lock-up-expiry price drop |

## Verified numbers (web + Carta, 2024–2025)

- **Employee equity by hire order (Carta, 9,000+ initial grants to a startup's first 10 hires,
  2024–2025 data):** hire #1 median **1.5%** of fully diluted shares (25th pct 0.5%, 75th pct 4%);
  hire #2 ~0.85%; by hire #5–6 ~0.3–0.33%. First engineer specifically: median ~2.0%. Broad bands:
  hires #1–5 → 0.5–2%, #6–20 → 0.1–0.5%, #21+ → 0.01–0.1%. ✅ (Carta "State of Startup Compensation"
  H1/H2 2024, H1/H2 2025.)
- **Vesting standard:** 4-year vest, 1-year cliff — industry-standard since the 1980s (Y Combinator/
  NVCA norm); leave before the cliff, keep nothing. ✅
- **Funding-stage valuations & dilution per round** — reused from `founder.md` (already verified there,
  Carta 2024 data): pre-seed ~$6M post-money; seed median **$14.8M** (rounded to $15M on-screen);
  Series A median post-money **$78.7M**; median dilution per round ≈ seed 19.5%, Series A 18%,
  Series B 14%, Series C 10%. ✅
- **ISO / AMT "tax bomb":** exercising Incentive Stock Options and holding (not selling same-year)
  creates a "phantom gain" — the spread between strike price and current fair-market value — that
  counts toward Alternative Minimum Taxable Income even though no cash changed hands. A real, commonly
  cited trap for pre-IPO employees who exercise early. ✅ (Secfi, NCEO, Kitces, Chase — consistent
  across sources.)
- **IPO lock-up period:** standard underwriter lock-up is **180 days** — employees and insiders are
  contractually barred from selling shares for six months after the IPO, however high the stock trades
  on day one. ✅ (Standard across nearly all US IPOs; well documented.)
- **Lock-up-expiry price effect:** stocks commonly see selling pressure and price declines when a
  lock-up expires and a large new supply of shares becomes sellable at once — a well-documented pattern
  (widely covered in IPO literature/financial press). ✅ directional (magnitude varies by company).
- **Startup failure / option worthlessness:** commonly cited that **~75%** of venture-backed startups
  fail to return cash to investors (Shikhar Ghosh, HBS — also cited in `founder.md`); a seed-stage
  company's odds of a **$100M+ exit are ~3.2%**. The large majority of granted options are never worth
  anything. ✅ (widely repeated figures, consistent across sources.)
- **Secondary "tender offer" at unicorn stage:** a well-established, real practice — late-stage private
  "unicorns" periodically run structured tender offers letting employees sell a capped slice (commonly
  10–20%) of VESTED shares for cash before any IPO, precisely because staying private longer left
  employees illiquid for years. ✅ directional (terms vary company to company; no single universal %).
- **Liquidation preference:** a real, standard term in preferred-stock financings — investors in a round
  (especially a down round) are contractually paid back FIRST (often 1x, sometimes "participating") in
  any sale/liquidation before common stockholders (employees, and often founders) see anything. This is
  the real mechanism that can make a big "valuation" number pay common stockholders $0. ✅

## Real mechanics / jargon woven into the story

- **Cap table** — the ledger of who owns what; common stock (employees) sits below preferred
  (investors) in payout order.
- **Vesting cliff** — nothing is truly "yours" for the first year; leave at month 11, keep $0.
- **409A valuation** — the independent appraisal that sets your OPTION strike price; a down round
  usually forces a lower 409A, which can leave earlier option grants "underwater" relative to newer,
  cheaper ones.
- **Golden handcuffs** — the vesting schedule itself becomes a reason to stay somewhere that's stopped
  being good for you.
- **Liquidation preference (1x, participating)** — see above; the clause that turns "the company sold
  for $400M" into "you got nothing."
- **Down round / cram-down** — a later round priced BELOW the last one; existing (especially
  non-participating) holders get diluted hardest, sometimes via anti-dilution ratchets that mint the
  new investors extra shares at the old holders' expense.
- **Tender offer / secondary sale** — a company-sanctioned window to sell some vested shares for cash
  while still private; the only liquidity most employees see before an exit.
- **ISO vs. NSO, AMT, 83(b) election** — the tax maze around exercising options; the "tax bomb" of
  owing real money on a paper gain.
- **Lock-up period (180 days)** — post-IPO, contractually can't sell even though the stock is trading
  and the number is right there on the screen.

## Moral-erosion arc (employee, not founder)

recruited true believer (equity as a lottery ticket, below-market pay) → the believer who stops
questioning the pitch → the person who has to fire the people they hired, because growth-at-all-costs
said so → the one who learns the number on the cap table and the number in a bank account are not the
same thing → the survivor of a down round who stays anyway (the cliff, the sunk years) → the one who
finally gets a WIRE, not a spreadsheet → the newly, technically, unbelievably rich person who still
can't touch it → the person who understands, finally, that "unicorn" was never a promise about what
you'd keep.

## The loop (open + payoff)

Open on the AFTERMATH: the day after a layoff round, an empty desk row, two folded papers in your
hand — a severance list and your own equity grant letter, both just paper. Close on you handing a NEW
hire her own folded grant letter in a much smaller, much newer garage, and saying the exact line your
own recruiter said to you at Level 1 — the door stays open; the machine finds someone new to become
hire #1. Priya (the founder who recruited you) is already texting about "a new idea" — the unresolved
universe thread for a future episode.
