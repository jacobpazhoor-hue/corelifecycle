# Research: Black Market Surgeon (format: scenario)

Proof: "Hypothetically" channel's "What It's Like to Be a Black Market Surgeon" — 5.3M views (their #1
video). Format is `scenario` — NOT a job-rank ladder. It's a single escalating situation: a licensed
surgeon falls out of the legal system and rises through the underground economy of off-book medicine.
Levels are escalating NIGHTS/CONTRACTS/$ — the "rank" is how deep into the black market you go, not a
job title.

## The arc (grounded in real underground-medicine patterns)

**How doctors actually end up here:** license revocation is the real on-ramp. State medical boards
strip licenses for malpractice, addiction, or patient-safety violations (verified: Ohio board
permanently revoked Dr. Katharine Grawe — "Dr. Roxy" — after botched TikTok-famous cosmetic procedures
injured three patients). Once delicensed, going back to *any* paid medicine is itself a felony/misdemeanor
depending on state — which is what pushes a revoked doctor into cash-only, off-book work rather than
a legitimate second career.

**Level 1 — the unlicensed cosmetic economy ("pumping parties").** Real and well-documented: unlicensed
injectors charge cash to inject silicone (often industrial-grade) or substitutes — liquid paraffin,
tire sealant, cement glue — into breasts/lips/buttocks in hotel rooms, non-medical settings. Verified
cases: Philadelphia's "Black Madam" (Padge Victoria Windsor) convicted after a client died from a
silicone injection; Fiordaliza Pichardo died of a pulmonary silicone embolism the day after an injection.
A reviewed case series of 44 pulmonary-silicone-embolism patients found roughly 1 in 4 died. In 2023,
Houston police arrested Alexander Padilla — never licensed by the Texas Medical Board — in an undercover
sting; he'd quoted an informant $7,500 for breast surgery, $1,000 deposit up front. **On-screen $:
$1,000–$7,500 per procedure**, cash, no medical record.

**Level 2 — the gunshot-wound trade.** US law (e.g., NY Penal Law §265.25 and equivalent statutes in
most states) makes it a mandatory-reporting crime for any treating physician to NOT report a gunshot or
stab wound to police — failure is a chargeable misdemeanor (real hospitals have been investigated for
this, e.g., the NY-Presbyterian/Plaxico Burress case). This is the exact real-world mechanism that
creates demand for an off-book "patch-up" doctor: anyone who can't afford the police finding out pays
cash for someone who won't report. **On-screen $: $300–$1,500 per gunshot wound, no chart, no name.**

**Level 3 — the standing retainer.** Once a local dealer/crew keeps coming back, real underground-clinic
patterns (per journalistic/DOJ sting reporting on unlicensed clinics) shift from per-job cash to a
monthly retainer for guaranteed availability — the doctor becomes "on call" for one crew.
**On-screen $: ~$5,000/month retainer** (dramatized composite — the retainer figure itself is not
independently documented and should be read as illustrative, not sourced).

**Level 4 — organ brokering, the real ceiling of the trade.** Documented at scale: a 2017 Global
Financial Integrity report estimated illegal organ trafficking generates **$840M–$1.7B annually**,
with roughly 12,000 illegal transplants performed worldwide in 2014 (two-thirds kidneys). Black-market
kidney prices to the buyer run **$50,000–$200,000**; the actual donor — often trafficked, coerced, or
desperate — typically receives **under 10% of that, often just $1,000–$10,000**. This gap (broker takes
the overwhelming majority) is the sharpest real moral-cost fact in the whole space and the natural
midpoint-reversal material: the "surgeon" isn't paid for surgery, he's paid to make trafficking look
medical. **On-screen $: $250,000 buyer-side fee per organ; flag the donor-cut disparity explicitly as
the "wait, that's real?" share-beat.**

**Level 5 — syndicate house doctor.** The apex a black-market surgeon can reach: exclusive, on-retainer
physician to a criminal organization, with real equipment, an actual private clinic, guards — and zero
ability to leave (knowing what he knows). This tier is dramatized/composite (no single documented case
matches it exactly — flagged as UNVERIFIED specifics, though it is a consistent pattern across cartel-
adjacent reporting on clandestine clinics). **On-screen $: ~$1,000,000/year**, at the cost of total
captivity.

## Numbers spine (the on-screen $ escalation)
$0 (license revoked, debt) → $1,000–$7,500/procedure (pumping parties) → $300–$1,500/gunshot wound →
$5,000/month retainer → $250,000 organ-broker fee → $1,000,000/year syndicate retainer, at the cost of
never being able to walk away.

## Flags (unverified / dramatized)
- The $5,000/month "crew retainer" and the $1,000,000/year "syndicate house doctor" tier are dramatized
  composites, not independently sourced figures — grounded in the *pattern* of documented cases (sting
  operations, cartel-adjacent reporting) but not a specific cited case.
- Cartel/mob "private clinic" doctors are a consistent trope in journalism about organized crime but
  this research did not find one single, citable, on-the-record case with financial specifics — treat
  as dramatized cautionary fiction, never how-to.
