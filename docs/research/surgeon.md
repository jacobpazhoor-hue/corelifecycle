# Research — Every Level of a Surgeon

The on-screen spine is the comp escalation from a debt-financed medical student to the people who
price the entire system above medicine. All figures are US, 2024–2025 data. Verified figures are
cited; FLAGGED items are estimates.

## The real career ladder (training is brutally long: ~13–16 years from day one)

| # | Level | Real title / stage | Comp (on-screen) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | The Medical Student | MS1–MS4 (4 yrs) | **$0 (−$235K debt)** | You PAY to be here |
| 2 | The Intern | PGY-1 resident | **$68,000** | First year on the wards |
| 3 | The Resident | PGY-2→5, then Chief Resident | **$77,000** | General surgery is 5 yrs |
| 4 | The Fellow | subspecialty fellow (1–3 yrs) | **$90,000** | Still trainee pay at ~30+ |
| 5 | The Attending | board-certified general surgeon | **$475,000** | First real salary, ~age 33 |
| 6 | The Specialist | neurosurgeon / high-volume subspecialist | **$960,000** | Volume + RVUs |
| 7 | The Chief of Surgery | dept chair / surgeon-executive | **$1,500,000** | Stops operating, runs throughput |
| 8 | The Architect | the system that prices care | **$5,300,000,000,000** | Above medicine entirely |

## Verified numbers (web, 2025–2026)

- **PGY-1 resident:** national average **$68,166**; median $66,986 (AAMC 2025 Survey of Resident/
  Fellow Stipends). Surgical residents are paid the same as other specialties at each PGY level.
  Salary rises ~$2–3K per post-graduate year; PGY-5/chief ≈ **$77K**. ✅
- **General surgeon:** median total comp **~$482K** (Doximity 2024) to **$546K** (SalaryDr 2026);
  MGMA in the ~$450–500K band. On-screen: **$475K**. ✅
- **Neurosurgeon (highest-paid specialty):** MGMA median total comp **$962,912**; SalaryDr median
  **$900K**; range $442K → $875K+. Thoracic ~$750K, orthopedic ~$749K, orthopedic-trauma median
  $668K (75th pct $975K, Yale/MGMA DataDive). On-screen specialist: **$960K**. ✅
- **US National Health Expenditure 2024:** **$5.3 trillion**, +7.2% YoY, **18.0% of GDP**,
  $15,474 per person (CMS). Hospitals $1.63T; physician & clinical services $1.11T; prescription
  drugs $467B; private insurance $1.64T; Medicare $1.12T; Medicaid $932B. On-screen apex: **$5.3T**. ✅

## FLAGGED (estimates — directional, not from a single citation)

- **Median medical-school debt ≈ $235K** (AAMC historically ~$200–250K for those with debt). ⚠️
- **Chief of Surgery / department chair $1.5M:** academic chairs typically combine a clinical salary,
  administrative stipend, and bonus; total $1–2M is plausible at large systems. Directional. ⚠️
- **Hospital-system CEO comp** ($10M+ at the largest non-profits) used only as narrative color in
  Level 8, not as the on-screen number. ⚠️
- 80-hour resident workweek = the ACGME cap (real, since 2003); "1 in 3" wash-out and specific
  fellowship lengths vary by specialty. ⚠️

## Real mechanics / jargon to ground the script

- **The match / NRMP** — algorithm that binds graduating students to residency programs.
- **PGY-N** — "post-graduate year," how rank is measured in training.
- **Scut work** — the menial labor of an intern (notes, labs, pages).
- **RVU (relative value unit)** — Medicare's productivity currency; an attending's pay is tied to
  wRVUs generated. The lever that turns medicine into volume.
- **Attending / fellow / resident / intern** — the operating hierarchy in any OR.
- **CPT codes & reimbursement** — what each procedure is "worth" is set by CMS + the AMA RUC, not
  by the surgeon. This is the hinge of Level 8: someone above medicine prices the cut.
- **Service line** — a hospital's profit center (cardiac, ortho). Unprofitable lines (rural trauma,
  obstetrics) get closed by executives, not clinicians.
- **Locum tenens** — traveling contract surgery, often very high day-rates.
- **The oath** — the white-coat ceremony / Hippocratic oath bookends the story (the loop).

## Moral-erosion arc (the spine under the money)

earnest healer (the oath) → exhausted intern (first death, learns to detach) → competent resident
("the gallbladder in bed 4") → technical master (the hands) → owner of the outcome (RVUs appear) →
volume operator (does this patient need surgery, or can they be billed?) → administrator (closes the
unprofitable trauma unit) → the architect who decides what a life is worth. Healer → technician →
operator → administrator → the system that prices lives.
